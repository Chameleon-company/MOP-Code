Introduction / Background¶

Cities like Melbourne are investing in bike-sharing programs as part of broader efforts to promote sustainable transportation, reduce traffic congestion, and encourage healthy lifestyles. However, the adoption and usage of bikes vary significantly based on external factors such as weather, time of day, and existing traffic conditions. Understanding these dynamics is essential for developing smarter infrastructure, informing policy decisions, and optimizing urban mobility systems.

This use case focuses on analyzing and correlating bike usage with traffic and weather patterns using the following datasets:

  1. Bike Share Dock Locations:

Dataset API Link - /api/explore/v2.1/catalog/datasets/traffic-count-vehicle-classification-2014-2017/records?limit=-1

Provides the geolocations and metadata of bike docking stations across Melbourne, useful for mapping infrastructure distribution.

  1. Super Sunday Bike Count:

Dataset API Link - /api/explore/v2.1/catalog/datasets/bike-share-dock-locations/records?limit=-1

Captures periodic counts of bike riders at various intersections on key days, offering insights into bike usage trends across times and routes.

  1. Traffic Count & Vehicle Classification (2014–2017): Dataset API Link - /api/explore/v2.1/catalog/datasets/super-sunday-bike-count/records?limit=-1

Contains detailed traffic data across multiple Melbourne roads, classified by vehicle type, helping to assess road congestion and vehicle usage over time.

By integrating these datasets, this use case aims to identify key factors influencing bike usage, compare cycling trends with car usage under varying weather and traffic conditions, and inform evidence-based decision-making for city planning and transport development.

def load_data_from_api(url):
    response = requests.get(url)
    if response.status_code == 200:
        data = response.json()
        records = data.get('results', [])
        return pd.DataFrame(records)
    else:
        print(f"Failed to fetch data from {url}")
        return pd.DataFrame()

Conclusion¶

This project provided valuable insights into urban mobility patterns in Melbourne through the integration of bike usage data, traffic counts, and geospatial mapping. By loading, cleaning, and visualizing datasets from multiple sources, we were able to uncover correlations between vehicle types and speed, distinguish between weekday and weekend traffic behaviors, and highlight trends in public engagement with cycling initiatives such as the Super Sunday bike event. The Folium-based geospatial map further enabled us to identify gaps in bike dock infrastructure. These analyses collectively support data-driven decisions for city planners aiming to enhance sustainable transportation systems, reduce traffic congestion, and promote cycling as a viable mode of commute. The findings highlight the importance of temporal and spatial factors in traffic dynamics and reinforce the value of open data for urban development.

Recommendations:¶

  • Traffic Management: Authorities should monitor suburbs with recurring congestion (low average speeds) and consider dynamic traffic light systems or rerouting strategies.

  • Infrastructure Prioritization: High-traffic vehicle classes should be used to prioritize which roads or suburbs require expansion or maintenance.

  • Cycling Infrastructure: Invest further in areas with high bike dock usage during events like Super Sunday, suggesting latent cycling demand.

  • Data Integration: Combine traffic and weather data to improve prediction accuracy and planning responsiveness.

  • Public Awareness: Share insights with the public, such as peak congestion times or high-cycling corridors, to encourage behavior change.

Limitations¶

  • Incomplete Correlation Matrix: Some vehicle classes (8–13) had missing or unusable data, reducing the depth of full traffic composition analysis.

  • Missing Time Granularity: Without hourly-level timestamps, fine-grained traffic pattern analysis (e.g., rush hour) couldn't be done.

  • Outlier Sensitivity: Average speeds may have been influenced by data noise or outliers, which need additional preprocessing or smoothing.

  • Spatial Gaps: Folium maps revealed clusters but lacked density-based coloring; heatmaps could further enhance spatial understanding.

  • No Weather or Event Context: External factors like rain, construction, or public events weren’t integrated, limiting real-world impact analysis.

Next Steps¶

  • Perform hourly or seasonal trend analysis for deeper temporal insights (e.g., morning vs evening traffic).

  • Use clustering to group suburbs by traffic profiles and recommend tailored interventions per group.

  • Apply machine learning (e.g., Random Forest) to predict traffic volume or congestion likelihood based on vehicle mix.

  • Incorporate weather and event datasets to model real-world influences on traffic and bike usage.

  • Build an interactive dashboard (using Plotly Dash or Power BI) for stakeholders to explore traffic and mobility patterns in real time.
